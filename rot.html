Reign of Terror


The Reign of Terror was a period of violence and mass executions of opposition to the French Revolution. It was caused by a rivalry of two political groups, the Jacobins (radical republicans) and Girodins (moderate republicans) and sanctioned by Maximilien Robespierre of the Committee of Public Safety. Many of those executed by guillotine, without trial, were members of the aristocracy, clergy, or the bourgeoisie, including Marie Antionette and Olympe de Gouges. Other events such as the execution of King Louis XVI, the rise of the Committee of Public Safety, and the passage of laws enabling revolutionary tribunals contributed to the atmosphere of fear and suspicion.
While the Terror was initially justified as a means of defending the Revolution from internal and external threats, it ultimately undermined the very ideals it sought to uphold. The excessive violence and disregard for due process during the Terror provoked backlash both domestically and internationally, contributing to the downfall of Robespierre and the radical Jacobins. Moreover, the Reign of Terror served as a cautionary tale about the dangers of unchecked power and the erosion of individual liberties in the pursuit of ideological purity.
