August 14th, 1789-

Storming of the Bastille


This was the first real act of the revolution. Louis XVI was secretly assembling troops to crush the National Assembly. However, this plot was discovered, and a rebel group called the San-Culottes stormed the Bastille. Fueled by economic hardships, political discontent, and rumors of royalist conspiracy, members of the National Assembly as well as many other commoner citizens stormed the Bastille. The Bastille was an armory and a prison with prisoners who were against Louis XVI, and it symbolized the tyranny of the Bourbon monarchy. The guy in charge of the Bastille, Marquis de Launay, refused to surrender the prison which led to fighting between the mob and the soldiers. He ordered the troops to open fire. However, the crowd managed to breach the outer defenses of the Bastille. They freed all the prisoners and stole weapons and armor. They needed protection from the soldiers. They also took the warden hostage, who was then later killed in the aftermath. The people cut off the heads of the soldiers and put them on pitchforks as a sign not to mess with them as they walked through Paris.
It marked the beginning of the French Revolution's more radical phase, characterized by the dismantling of the old order and the pursuit of republican ideals. The fall of the Bastille was celebrated as a triumph of the people over despotism and became a potent symbol of popular resistance to royal authority. This revolutionary act inspired widespread uprisings and the formation of local militias known as "National Guards". However it prompted King Louis XVI to be afraid of the riots and political opposition which led him to make concessions to the revolutionary movement and to recognize the authority of the newly formed National Assembly.
