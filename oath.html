June 20, 1789-

Tennis Court Oath


In Versailles, members of the Third Estate were excluded from the Estates General meeting due to disputes over voting, and they were locked out of the meeting. After not receiving adequate financial reforms or changes within the Convention of the Estates-General, The Third Estate and their leaders like Robespierre and Mirabeau, took an oath in a nearby indoor tennis court near the meeting hall to never separate until a written constitution had been instituted in France.
This oath symbolized the determination of the Third Estate to challenge the authority of the monarchy and to assert its role in shaping the future of France. The Tennis Court Oath marked a significant turning point in the Revolutions because it demonstrated the growing unity and resolve among the deputies and paved the way for the abolition of absolute monarch and the establishment of a constitutional order.
The First and Second Estate later joined the National Assembly after capitulation from Louis XVI.
